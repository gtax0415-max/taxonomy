# Student Loan Interest Deduction
## Description
Deduction for interest paid on a qualified student loan for higher education, taken as an adjustment to income.
## Notes
- Maximum deduction is $2,500 per return per year (not per loan and not per person)
- Form 1098-E Box 1 reports the interest paid; lenders issue it when interest is $600 or more, but interest below that is still deductible
- The deduction phases out at higher MAGI and is COMPLETELY UNAVAILABLE for married filing separately — not reduced, but barred entirely regardless of income
- 2026 phase-out ranges (Rev. Proc. 2025-32): single, head of household, or qualifying surviving spouse $85,000-$100,000; married filing jointly $175,000-$205,000. For 2025 the joint range was $170,000-$200,000; the single range was the same
- The $2,500 cap is NOT indexed for inflation, but the phase-out thresholds are
- You cannot claim it if you are claimed as a dependent on someone else's return
- The loan must have been for qualified education expenses for you, your spouse, or a dependent
- Available whether you take the standard deduction or itemize
## Required Information
- Form 1098-E Box 1 (student loan interest received by lender)
- MAGI (for the phase-out)
- Filing status
## Questions
- Did you pay interest on a student loan this year?
- Did you receive a Form 1098-E?
- Are you filing Married Filing Separately (which disqualifies the deduction)?
- Are you claimed as a dependent by someone else?
## Common Errors
- Claiming more than the $2,500 maximum
- Claiming it while Married Filing Separately
- Missing the deduction because no Form 1098-E arrived (under $600 of interest is still deductible)
- Deducting principal payments instead of only interest
## Prompt
- I paid student loan interest this year and got a 1098-E.
- I am paying off student loans and want to deduct the interest.
