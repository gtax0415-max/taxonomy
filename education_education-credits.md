# Education Credits — American Opportunity and Lifetime Learning
## Description
Two credits for higher-education costs, both claimed on Form 8863. You cannot claim both for the SAME student in the same year, so the choice matters.
## American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)
- Up to $2,500 per STUDENT per year: 100% of the first $2,000 of qualified expenses plus 25% of the next $2,000
- 40% REFUNDABLE, up to $1,000, which lands on Form 1040 Line 29. The other 60% is nonrefundable
- Available only for the first FOUR YEARS of postsecondary education, and only four tax years per student
- The student must be enrolled at least HALF TIME in a program leading to a degree or credential
- The student must not have a felony drug conviction
- Phases out at modified AGI of $80,000 to $90,000 (single) or $160,000 to $180,000 (married filing jointly). These are NOT indexed
- Qualified expenses include tuition, required fees, AND required course materials such as books, even if bought elsewhere
## Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC)
- Up to $2,000 per RETURN (not per student): 20% of up to $10,000 of qualified expenses
- Fully NONREFUNDABLE
- No limit on the number of years, no half-time requirement, no degree requirement. Available for a single course to improve job skills
- Phases out at the same modified AGI ranges as the AOTC
- Qualified expenses are tuition and required fees only — books generally do NOT count unless paid to the institution as a condition of enrollment
## Which one to use
The AOTC is worth more and is partially refundable, so use it when the student qualifies. The LLC covers graduate school, part-time study, and years five and beyond, where the AOTC is unavailable.
## New for 2026: SSN requirement
Beginning in 2026, a valid Social Security number is required to claim either the American Opportunity Credit or the Lifetime Learning Credit.
## Coordination rules
- You cannot claim a credit for expenses paid with tax-free scholarship, grant, or 529 plan earnings — reduce qualified expenses by Form 1098-T Box 5 amounts first
- You cannot claim both the AOTC and the LLC for the same student in the same year
- If a student is claimed as a dependent, only the person claiming the dependent may take the credit, regardless of who paid
- Married filing separately cannot claim either credit
## Required Information
- Form 1098-T from each institution: Box 1 payments received, Box 5 scholarships or grants
- The school's name and EIN
- Receipts for required books and course materials (AOTC only)
- Number of prior years the AOTC has been claimed for that student
- Enrollment status and year in the program
## Questions
- Did you, your spouse, or a dependent pay qualified tuition?
- Did you receive Form 1098-T, and what is in Box 1 and Box 5?
- Is the student in the first four years and enrolled at least half time (AOTC), or beyond that (LLC)?
- How many years has the AOTC already been claimed for this student?
- Were expenses paid with scholarships or a 529 distribution?
- Is your modified AGI above $80,000 ($160,000 joint)?
## Common Errors
- Claiming the AOTC for a fifth year or for a graduate student
- Failing to reduce qualified expenses by tax-free scholarships in Form 1098-T Box 5
- Double-dipping with 529 plan distributions on the same expenses
- Claiming both credits for the same student
- A dependent student claiming the credit when the parent claims the exemption
- Claiming books for the Lifetime Learning Credit when they were not paid to the school
## Prompt
- I paid college tuition and got a 1098-T.
- My daughter is a sophomore in college.
- I took a class to improve my job skills.
