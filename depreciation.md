# Depreciation and Section 179
## Description
Deduction for the cost of business property with a useful life of more than one year — equipment, computers, furniture, vehicles, and improvements. Figured on Form 4562, which about 10.3 million returns file (TY 2022).
## Three ways to recover the cost
1. REGULAR DEPRECIATION (MACRS) — spread the cost over the property's recovery period (5 years for computers and vehicles, 7 years for furniture, 39 years for nonresidential real property)
2. SECTION 179 EXPENSING — deduct the full cost in the year placed in service. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act raised the limit to $2,500,000 with a $4,000,000 phase-out threshold for 2025 (up from $1,250,000 and $3,130,000), and both amounts are indexed for inflation for 2026 and later — confirm the current year figures before filing. The deduction phases out dollar-for-dollar once total purchases exceed the threshold. Section 179 CANNOT create a net loss — it is limited to taxable business income, and the excess carries forward. Heavy SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR have a separate, much lower Section 179 cap (roughly $30,000, indexed annually)
3. BONUS DEPRECIATION — 100% first-year deduction, permanently restored by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025. Unlike Section 179, bonus depreciation CAN create a loss and has no dollar cap
## Notes
- Property must be used more than 50% for business to qualify for Section 179
- Applies to new AND used property, as long as it is new to you
- Passenger automobiles remain subject to the Section 280F annual dollar caps even with 100% bonus depreciation; SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR have a separate Section 179 cap
- Section 179 and bonus depreciation both accelerate deductions, but they interact differently with losses — Section 179 is limited by income, bonus depreciation is not
- Claiming Section 179 or bonus depreciation on a vehicle prevents using the standard mileage rate for that vehicle in later years
- Depreciation is recaptured as ordinary income if the asset is sold for more than its depreciated basis (Form 4797)

## Required Information
- Description, cost, and date placed in service for each asset
- Business use percentage
- Total cost of all property placed in service during the year (for the Section 179 phase-out)
- Prior-year depreciation schedules
- Form 4562
## Questions
- Did you buy equipment, computers, furniture, or vehicles for the business?
- What was the cost and when was each item placed in service?
- What percentage of use is business?
- Is the business profitable (Section 179 is limited by income)?
- Do you want the full deduction now or spread over several years?
## Common Errors
- Deducting the full cost of long-lived property as a supply expense instead of depreciating it
- Claiming Section 179 in excess of business income (it cannot create a loss)
- Ignoring the passenger auto depreciation caps
- Missing the more-than-50% business use requirement
- Overlooking depreciation recapture on sale
## Prompt
- I bought equipment for my business this year.
- I purchased a computer and furniture for my company.
- I want to write off a vehicle I bought for work.
