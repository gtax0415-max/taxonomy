# Form 8606 — Nondeductible IRAs
## Description
Tracks the after-tax BASIS in traditional IRAs so that money already taxed once is not taxed again when distributed. Also reports Roth conversions and certain Roth distributions. About 3.25 million returns file it (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## Why it matters
If you contribute to a traditional IRA but cannot deduct it (because of the income limits — see federal/deductions/adjustments/ira-deduction.md), that contribution is AFTER-TAX money. Form 8606 records it as basis. Without the form, the IRS has no record of the basis, and the entire distribution later gets taxed — meaning you pay tax twice on the same dollars.
## The three parts
- Part I: nondeductible contributions to traditional IRAs and the taxable amount of distributions from traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs
- Part II: conversions from traditional, SEP, or SIMPLE IRAs to Roth IRAs
- Part III: distributions from Roth IRAs
## The pro-rata rule — the most misunderstood part
You cannot choose to withdraw only the after-tax portion. Every distribution or conversion is treated as coming proportionally from basis and pre-tax money, based on the TOTAL of ALL your traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs at year end.
Example: $7,000 of basis in $70,000 of total IRA value means 10% of any distribution is tax-free and 90% is taxable — even if you convert exactly the $7,000 you just contributed. This is what limits the "backdoor Roth" strategy for people who already hold pre-tax IRA money.
## Notes
- Basis carries forward year to year, so prior-year Forms 8606 are essential records — keep them indefinitely
- Roth IRA contributions themselves are not reported here, but Roth CONVERSIONS and certain Roth distributions are
- Employer plan (401(k)) after-tax basis is tracked separately and is not part of the IRA pro-rata calculation
- A penalty can apply for failing to file Form 8606 when required

## Required Information
- Nondeductible contributions made for the year
- Total basis from all prior Forms 8606
- Total value of ALL traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs on December 31
- Distributions and conversions during the year (Form 1099-R)
## Questions
- Did you make a traditional IRA contribution you could not deduct?
- Do you have basis from prior nondeductible contributions?
- Did you convert any traditional IRA money to a Roth?
- What was the total value of all your traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs at December 31?
## Common Errors
- Not filing Form 8606 for a nondeductible contribution, causing the same money to be taxed twice at distribution
- Assuming a backdoor Roth conversion is tax-free when other pre-tax IRA money exists (the pro-rata rule applies)
- Losing prior-year Forms 8606 and thereby losing the basis record
- Applying the pro-rata rule to only one IRA instead of the total of all traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs
## Prompt
- I made a nondeductible IRA contribution.
- I did a backdoor Roth conversion.
- I have after-tax money in my traditional IRA.
