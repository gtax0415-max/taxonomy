# Trump Account
## Description
A new type of custodial TRADITIONAL IRA for children under 18, created by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act under IRC Section 530A. Accounts became available July 4, 2026. It is neither a credit nor a deduction — it is a savings account whose tax consequences arrive years later, at distribution.
## Who can contribute — anyone, including the self-employed
ANYONE may contribute to a child's Trump Account: a parent, a grandparent, a friend, an employer, a government, or a charity. A SELF-EMPLOYED parent with no Form W-2 can absolutely contribute, up to the same $5,000 annual cap as everyone else.
The source_doc field below lists only Form W-2 Box 12 code TA. That is NOT because employees are the only ones who can fund an account. It is because INDIVIDUAL CONTRIBUTIONS PRODUCE NO TAX DOCUMENT AT ALL — they are not deductible, not reported on the return, and generate no information return. A self-employed parent who contributes $5,000 has made a perfectly valid contribution and has nothing to put on the return.
## The employer route (Section 128) and self-employment
The $2,500 employer contribution is a separate track with its own rules, and this is where self-employment status matters:
- W-2 EMPLOYEE: your employer may contribute up to $2,500 under a written Trump Account Contribution Program. Excluded from Box 1, identified in Box 12 code TA
- S-CORPORATION OWNER who takes W-2 wages: you are an employee of your own corporation, so the business may adopt a TACP and contribute for your dependent children — deductible to the business and tax-free to you. With no other employees there is no one the program could discriminate against
- SOLE PROPRIETOR or PARTNER with no W-2 wages: UNSETTLED. Section 129 (dependent care) explicitly treats self-employed individuals as employees for program purposes, but Section 128 contains NO parallel provision. Until the IRS says otherwise, an unincorporated self-employed person should not assume they can pay themselves an employer contribution. Individual contributions up to $5,000 remain available either way
- A TACP must be a separate WRITTEN plan for the exclusive benefit of employees, with nondiscrimination, notice, and annual statement requirements borrowed from Section 129(d). If you have staff, you cannot run a program that covers only your own children
## What is and is not a source document
For tax year 2026 only ONE document reaches the return:
- Form W-2 Box 12 code TA — employer contributions. Already excluded from Box 1, so it is informational rather than an amount you add or subtract
Everything else is NOT a source document:
- The federal $1,000 pilot contribution generates NO information return. Treasury deposits it directly. It does not appear on a Form W-2, a Form 1099, or anywhere on the return
- Form 4547 is the ELECTION you FILE to open the account and claim the $1,000. It is a form you create, not one you receive
- Custodian account statements do not feed any line of any return for 2026, because individual contributions are neither deductible nor reported. Keep them regardless — they are the contribution record that will establish basis when distributions eventually begin
- Form 1099-R will apply eventually, but NOT for 2026. See the timing note below
## The $1,000 federal pilot contribution
- A one-time $1,000 Treasury contribution for U.S. citizen children born January 1, 2025 through December 31, 2028 who have a Social Security number
- Requires an ELECTION on Form 4547, "Trump Account Election(s)," filed with a return or through the portal at trumpaccounts.gov
- The election is made by someone who expects the child to be their qualifying child under Section 152(c) for that year
- Deposits begin no earlier than July 4, 2026
- The $1,000 does NOT count against the annual contribution cap
- The pilot contribution is NOT taxable when deposited, but it IS taxable when eventually withdrawn
- Children who are not eligible for the $1,000 can still have a Trump Account — they simply do not get the seed
## Contribution limits
- $5,000 per child per year from all individual sources combined, for 2026 and 2027, indexed in $100 increments after 2027
- Contributions cannot be made before July 4, 2026
- Anyone can contribute — parents, grandparents, friends — but NO ONE gets a deduction for it
- Exempt from the $5,000 cap: the federal $1,000 pilot contribution, qualified general contributions from governments or 501(c)(3) organizations, and rollovers
## Employer contributions — IRC Section 128
- An employer may contribute up to $2,500 per year to the Trump Account of an employee or an employee's dependent
- The limit is PER EMPLOYEE, not per dependent. An employee with three eligible children still gets $2,500 total
- EXCLUDED from the employee's gross income, and reported on Form W-2 Box 12 using new code TA
- Counts AGAINST the $5,000 annual cap, so a family contributing on its own must coordinate
- Deductible as a business expense for the employer
- Requires a written Trump Account Contribution Program (TACP) under Section 128, with nondiscrimination rules borrowed from the dependent care assistance rules of Section 129(d) — the program cannot favor highly compensated employees
## Investment restriction
Funds must be invested in low-cost mutual funds or ETFs tracking the S&P 500 or another index made up primarily of U.S. equities. There is no choice of individual securities.
## Distributions — none are possible for tax year 2026
Three rules combine to rule out any 2026 distribution:
1. Accounts could not be funded before July 4, 2026
2. To be eligible, the child must NOT have turned 18 before the end of the calendar year the election is made, so every 2026 account holder turns 18 in 2027 or later
3. Withdrawals cannot occur before January 1 of the calendar year the child turns 18
The earliest possible distribution is therefore JANUARY 1, 2027, for a child who was 17 at the end of 2026. Most account holders are many years away from any distribution.
When distributions do begin they arrive on Form 1099-R Box 1 (gross) and Box 2a (taxable), with the Box 7b IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checkbox marked, and land on Form 1040 Line 4a and Line 4b — see federal/income/retirement/1099-r.md
## Distribution rules
- Generally NO withdrawals before January 1 of the calendar year the child turns 18
- After that the account is treated as a regular TRADITIONAL IRA: distributions are ordinary income, and the 10% early distribution additional tax applies before age 59.5 unless an exception applies (education, first-time home purchase, disaster recovery, and others)
- Because contributions were never deducted, the tax character of distributions is layered and can be complex — basis tracking matters
- One special exit: in the year the beneficiary turns 17, the assets may be transferred to an ABLE account
## How this compares to a 529 plan
A 529 grows tax-free and comes out tax-free for qualified education costs. A Trump Account grows tax-DEFERRED and comes out as ORDINARY INCOME. The Trump Account's advantage is the free $1,000 and the possible employer contribution, not the tax treatment of growth.

## Required Information
- Child's name, Social Security number, and date of birth
- Whether the child is a U.S. citizen born 2025 through 2028 (for the $1,000)
- Confirmation that the Form 4547 or online election was made
- Form W-2 Box 12 code TA amounts, if an employer contributed
- Total contributions from all sources for the year, to test the $5,000 cap
## Questions
- Do you have a child under 18 with a Social Security number?
- Was the child born between January 1, 2025 and December 31, 2028 (eligible for the $1,000)?
- Have you filed Form 4547 or made the election online?
- Does your employer offer a Trump Account contribution program (Form W-2 Box 12 code TA)?
- Are you self-employed? You can still contribute individually, but the employer route depends on whether you take W-2 wages
- How much has been contributed from all sources this year?
## Common Errors
- Deducting a contribution — no individual deduction is allowed for anyone
- Forgetting that the employer's $2,500 counts against the same $5,000 cap
- Assuming the $2,500 employer limit is per child rather than per employee
- Treating it like a 529 and expecting tax-free withdrawals for education
- Missing the election entirely and forfeiting the $1,000 for an eligible child
- Attempting a withdrawal before the year the child turns 18
- Looking for a Form 1099 or other statement for the federal $1,000 — none is issued
- Expecting the $1,000 to appear on a Form W-2; Box 12 code TA reports employer money only
- Assuming a self-employed parent cannot contribute — they can, up to $5,000; the contribution simply produces no tax document
- A sole proprietor paying themselves a Section 128 employer contribution without W-2 wages, which the statute does not clearly permit

## Status note
Guidance is still developing. Notice 2025-68 (December 2, 2025) was the first guidance, proposed regulations followed in IR-2026-31 (March 6, 2026), and final regulations are expected during 2026. Open questions include nondiscrimination testing methods for a TACP, whether ERISA applies to employer programs, integration with a Section 125 cafeteria plan, and the treatment of unincorporated self-employed owners. Confirm current rules before relying on the employer route.
