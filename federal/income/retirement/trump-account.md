# Trump Account
## Description
A new type of custodial TRADITIONAL IRA for children under 18, created by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act under IRC Section 530A. Accounts became available July 4, 2026. It is neither a credit nor a deduction — it is a savings account whose tax consequences arrive years later, at distribution.
## The $1,000 federal pilot contribution
- A one-time $1,000 Treasury contribution for U.S. citizen children born January 1, 2025 through December 31, 2028 who have a Social Security number
- Requires an ELECTION on Form 4547, "Trump Account Election(s)," filed with a return or through the portal at trumpaccounts.gov
- The election is made by someone who expects the child to be their qualifying child under Section 152(c) for that year
- Deposits begin no earlier than July 4, 2026
- The $1,000 does NOT count against the annual contribution cap
- The pilot contribution is NOT taxable when deposited, but it IS taxable when eventually withdrawn
- Children who are not eligible for the $1,000 can still have a Trump Account — they simply do not get the seed
## Contribution limits
- $5,000 per child per year from all individual sources combined, for 2026 and 2027, indexed in $100 increments after 2027
- Contributions cannot be made before July 4, 2026
- Anyone can contribute — parents, grandparents, friends — but NO ONE gets a deduction for it
- Exempt from the $5,000 cap: the federal $1,000 pilot contribution, qualified general contributions from governments or 501(c)(3) organizations, and rollovers
## Employer contributions — IRC Section 128
- An employer may contribute up to $2,500 per year to the Trump Account of an employee or an employee's dependent
- The limit is PER EMPLOYEE, not per dependent. An employee with three eligible children still gets $2,500 total
- EXCLUDED from the employee's gross income, and reported on Form W-2 Box 12 using new code TA
- Counts AGAINST the $5,000 annual cap, so a family contributing on its own must coordinate
- Deductible as a business expense for the employer
- Requires a written Trump Account Contribution Program (TACP) under Section 128, with nondiscrimination rules borrowed from the dependent care assistance rules of Section 129(d) — the program cannot favor highly compensated employees
## Investment restriction
Funds must be invested in low-cost mutual funds or ETFs tracking the S&P 500 or another index made up primarily of U.S. equities. There is no choice of individual securities.
## Distributions
- Generally NO withdrawals before January 1 of the calendar year the child turns 18
- After that the account is treated as a regular TRADITIONAL IRA: distributions are ordinary income, and the 10% early distribution additional tax applies before age 59.5 unless an exception applies (education, first-time home purchase, disaster recovery, and others)
- Because contributions were never deducted, the tax character of distributions is layered and can be complex — basis tracking matters
- One special exit: in the year the beneficiary turns 17, the assets may be transferred to an ABLE account
## How this compares to a 529 plan
A 529 grows tax-free and comes out tax-free for qualified education costs. A Trump Account grows tax-DEFERRED and comes out as ORDINARY INCOME. The Trump Account's advantage is the free $1,000 and the possible employer contribution, not the tax treatment of growth.

## Required Information
- Child's name, Social Security number, and date of birth
- Whether the child is a U.S. citizen born 2025 through 2028 (for the $1,000)
- Form 4547 election, or confirmation of the online election
- Form W-2 Box 12 code TA amounts, if an employer contributed
- Total contributions from all sources for the year, to test the $5,000 cap
## Questions
- Do you have a child under 18 with a Social Security number?
- Was the child born between January 1, 2025 and December 31, 2028 (eligible for the $1,000)?
- Have you filed Form 4547 or made the election online?
- Does your employer offer a Trump Account contribution program (Form W-2 Box 12 code TA)?
- How much has been contributed from all sources this year?
## Common Errors
- Deducting a contribution — no individual deduction is allowed for anyone
- Forgetting that the employer's $2,500 counts against the same $5,000 cap
- Assuming the $2,500 employer limit is per child rather than per employee
- Treating it like a 529 and expecting tax-free withdrawals for education
- Missing the election entirely and forfeiting the $1,000 for an eligible child
- Attempting a withdrawal before the year the child turns 18
