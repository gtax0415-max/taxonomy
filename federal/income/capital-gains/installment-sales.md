# Form 6252 — Installment Sale Income
## Description
Reports gain from a sale where you receive at least one payment AFTER the year of sale. Instead of recognizing the whole gain up front, you recognize it proportionally as payments come in. About 465,500 returns file it (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## Who files this
About 465,500 returns file Form 6252 (IRS SOI, TY 2022) — uncommon, but it recurs in specific situations:
- SELLER-FINANCED REAL ESTATE, where the buyer pays the seller directly over several years
- SALE OF A BUSINESS paid out over time
- FARMLAND or ranch sales structured across multiple years
- Any property sale where at least one payment arrives in a year AFTER the year of sale
It applies to real property and business assets. It does NOT apply to inventory or to publicly traded securities.
## How it works
- Compute the GROSS PROFIT PERCENTAGE: gross profit divided by contract price
- Each year, that percentage of the PRINCIPAL received is taxable gain
- INTEREST received is separate ordinary interest income, reported on Schedule B and Form 1040 Line 2b
- A Form 6252 is filed for the year of sale AND for every later year a payment is received
## Important limits
- DEPRECIATION RECAPTURE cannot be deferred. All Section 1245 and 1250 recapture is taxed IN THE YEAR OF SALE, even if you receive no cash that year
- Installment treatment is NOT available for sales of inventory, or for publicly traded securities
- You may ELECT OUT of installment treatment and report the entire gain in the year of sale, which can make sense if you expect higher rates later or want to use expiring losses
- Interest may be owed on deferred tax for large installment obligations (generally over $5 million)
- Selling to a RELATED PARTY triggers special acceleration rules if that party resells within two years
## Required Information
- Selling price, cost basis, and selling expenses
- Contract price and gross profit percentage
- Principal and interest received each year
- Any depreciation recaptured in the year of sale
## Questions
- Did you sell property and receive payments over more than one year?
- What was the gross profit percentage?
- How much principal versus interest did you receive this year?
- Was there depreciation to recapture in the year of sale?
- Did you sell to a related party?
## Common Errors
- Deferring depreciation recapture (it must all be recognized in the year of sale)
- Treating the entire payment as gain instead of applying the gross profit percentage
- Forgetting to report the interest portion separately as interest income
- Failing to file Form 6252 in later years when payments continue
## Prompt
- I sold property and the buyer is paying me over several years.
- I have an installment sale from a prior year.
