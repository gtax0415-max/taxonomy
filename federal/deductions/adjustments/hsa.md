# HSA Deduction
## Description
Deduction for contributions you made to a Health Savings Account with your own after-tax money. Requires being covered by a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP).
## Notes
- Only contributions YOU made directly are deducted here. Contributions made through payroll (pre-tax) and employer contributions appear in Form W-2 Box 12 code W and are ALREADY excluded from Form W-2 Box 1 wages — do not deduct those again
- Contribution limits for 2026: $4,400 self-only coverage / $8,750 family coverage, plus a $1,000 catch-up if age 55 or older (the catch-up is fixed and not indexed). For 2025 the limits were $4,300 / $8,550
- To qualify, the HDHP for 2026 must have a minimum deductible of $1,700 self-only / $3,400 family, with out-of-pocket maximums no higher than $8,500 / $17,000
- Must be enrolled in an HDHP and not enrolled in Medicare or claimed as someone's dependent
- Contributions can be made up to the tax filing deadline for the prior year
- Figured on Form 8889, which must be attached
## Required Information
- Form 5498-SA (contributions made) and/or Form W-2 Box 12 code W
- Type of HDHP coverage (self-only or family)
- Months of HDHP coverage during the year
- Age (for the 55+ catch-up)
## Questions
- Were you covered by a High Deductible Health Plan, and for how many months?
- Was your coverage self-only or family?
- Did you contribute your own money outside of payroll deductions?
- Are you 55 or older (catch-up contribution)?
## Common Errors
- Deducting payroll/employer contributions again when they were already excluded from Form W-2 Box 1
- Contributing more than the limit (excess contributions are penalized)
- Claiming the deduction without HDHP coverage
- Forgetting Form 8889
## Prompt
- I contributed to my HSA this year.
- My employer contributed to my HSA and it shows on my W-2.
- I have a high deductible health plan and an HSA.
