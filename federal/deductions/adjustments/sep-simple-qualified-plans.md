# Self-Employed SEP, SIMPLE, and Qualified Plans
## Description
Deduction for retirement plan contributions made by a self-employed person for themselves — SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, and solo 401(k) or other qualified plans.
## Notes
- This is the SELF-EMPLOYED owner's own contribution. Contributions made for employees are a business expense on Schedule C instead
- SEP-IRA: generally up to 25% of net self-employment earnings, subject to an annual dollar cap ($72,000 for 2026; $70,000 for 2025)
- Solo 401(k): allows both an employee deferral and an employer contribution, often permitting a larger total than a SEP at lower income levels
- SIMPLE IRA: lower limits, with required employer matching
- The deduction is limited by net self-employment earnings after the deductible part of self-employment tax
- Deadlines vary by plan type; a SEP can generally be established and funded up to the extended filing deadline
- Available whether you take the standard deduction or itemize
## Related
- Traditional IRA contributions go on Schedule 1 Line 20 instead — see ira-deduction.md
- Deductible part of self-employment tax — see self-employment-tax-deduction.md
## Required Information
- Type of plan (SEP, SIMPLE, solo 401(k))
- Amount contributed for yourself
- Net self-employment earnings
## Questions
- Are you self-employed with a retirement plan for yourself?
- What type of plan is it?
- How much did you contribute for yourself (as opposed to for employees)?
- What were your net self-employment earnings?
## Common Errors
- Deducting employee contributions here instead of on Schedule C
- Contributing more than the percentage or dollar limit
- Confusing this with the traditional IRA deduction (Schedule 1 Line 20)
## Prompt
- I am self-employed and contributed to a SEP-IRA.
- I have a solo 401(k) for my business.
