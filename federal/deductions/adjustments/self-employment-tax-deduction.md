# Deductible Part of Self-Employment Tax
## Description
Deduction for one half of the self-employment tax you owe. This compensates for the fact that a self-employed person pays both the employee and employer halves of Social Security and Medicare tax, whereas an employee pays only half.
## Notes
- One of the MOST COMMON adjustments: about 23 million returns claim it (roughly 36% of all Schedule 1 filers, TY 2022)
- Automatically computed on Schedule SE Line 13 and carried to Schedule 1 Line 15 — there is no separate form to receive
- The self-employment tax rate is 15.3% (12.4% Social Security up to the wage base, plus 2.9% Medicare); you deduct half of it. The Social Security wage base is $184,500 for 2026; the 2.9% Medicare portion has no cap
- This deduction reduces INCOME TAX only. It does not reduce the self-employment tax itself
- Available whether you take the standard deduction or itemize
- Applies to net earnings from Schedule C, Schedule F, and partnership self-employment income; it does not apply to S-corporation shareholder distributions or passive rental income

## Required Information
- Net profit from self-employment (Schedule C Line 31, Schedule F, or Schedule K-1)
- Completed Schedule SE
## Questions
- Did you have net earnings from self-employment of $400 or more?
- Have you completed Schedule SE?
- Do you also have W-2 wages (which use up part of the Social Security wage base)?
## Common Errors
- Forgetting the deduction entirely when self-employment income is present
- Deducting the full self-employment tax instead of only half
- Expecting it to reduce the self-employment tax itself (it only reduces income tax)
- Claiming it on S-corporation distributions or passive rental income
## Prompt
- I am self-employed and paid self-employment tax.
- I have freelance income and filed Schedule SE.
