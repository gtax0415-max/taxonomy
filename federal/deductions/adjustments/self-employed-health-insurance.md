# Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction
## Description
Deduction for health, dental, and qualified long-term care insurance premiums paid by a self-employed person for themselves, their spouse, and dependents.
## Notes
- Available to sole proprietors (Schedule C), partners, and more-than-2% S-corporation shareholders
- Limited to the NET PROFIT from the business — you cannot deduct more than you earned from self-employment
- NOT available for any month you were eligible to participate in a subsidized health plan through your own or your spouse's employer
- This is an above-the-line adjustment, so it does not require itemizing. Premiums that do not qualify here may still be deductible as a medical expense on Schedule A (subject to the 7.5% AGI floor)
- Unlike other business expenses on Schedule C, this deduction does NOT reduce self-employment tax — it only reduces income tax
- If you bought Marketplace coverage with a premium tax credit, this deduction interacts with Form 8962 and requires a special calculation
## Required Information
- Premiums paid for health, dental, and long-term care insurance
- Net profit from self-employment
- Whether you or your spouse had access to an employer-subsidized plan (and in which months)
- Form 1095-A if coverage was through the Marketplace
## Questions
- Are you self-employed and paying for your own health insurance?
- What was your net profit from the business?
- Were you or your spouse eligible for an employer's health plan at any point in the year?
- Did you get coverage through the Marketplace with a premium tax credit?
## Common Errors
- Deducting more than the business net profit
- Claiming it for months when an employer plan was available
- Deducting it on Schedule C as a business expense instead of on Schedule 1 Line 17
- Double-counting premiums also claimed on Schedule A
## Prompt
- I am self-employed and pay for my own health insurance.
- I am a freelancer and bought a Marketplace plan.
