# Casualty and Theft Losses
## Description
Deduction for personal property losses from a sudden, unexpected event. Since the TCJA, personal casualty losses are deductible ONLY if attributable to a FEDERALLY DECLARED DISASTER.
## Notes
- Personal casualty and theft losses are NOT deductible unless the loss occurred in a federally declared disaster area — ordinary theft, fire, or accident losses generally do not qualify
- Two reductions apply to each qualifying loss:
  1. Subtract $100 per casualty event
  2. Subtract 10% of AGI from the total of all such losses
- Must reduce the loss by any insurance reimbursement received or expected; failing to file an insurance claim can disallow the deduction
- Figured on Form 4684, then carried to Schedule A Line 15
- Business and income-producing property losses follow different rules and are not limited this way
- A qualifying disaster loss may be claimed either in the year of the disaster OR on an amended return for the prior year, which can accelerate the refund
## Required Information
- FEMA disaster declaration number for the area
- Date and description of the event
- Adjusted basis of the property and its decline in value
- Insurance reimbursement received or expected
- Form 4684
## Questions
- Was your property damaged or destroyed in a federally declared disaster?
- Did you file an insurance claim, and what was reimbursed?
- What was the property's cost basis and the decrease in its value?
- Do you want to claim the loss in the disaster year or the prior year?
## Common Errors
- Claiming an ordinary theft or accident loss with no federal disaster declaration
- Forgetting the $100 per event and 10% of AGI reductions
- Not subtracting expected insurance reimbursement
- Failing to file an insurance claim before deducting
## Prompt
- My home was damaged in a hurricane that was federally declared a disaster.
- I lost property in a wildfire this year.
