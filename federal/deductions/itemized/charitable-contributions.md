# Charitable Contributions
## Description
Donations to qualified charitable organizations. The rules changed significantly for tax year 2026 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, for both itemizers and non-itemizers.
## For 2026 and later: two important changes
1. NON-ITEMIZERS can now deduct cash contributions — up to $1,000 (single) or $2,000 (married filing jointly). This sits on top of the standard deduction. Cash gifts only; not available for donor-advised funds or private non-operating foundations. Not indexed for inflation
2. ITEMIZERS face a new 0.5% AGI FLOOR — only contributions exceeding 0.5% of AGI are deductible. Example: AGI $200,000 means the first $1,000 of giving is not deductible
Additionally, taxpayers in the top 37% bracket receive only a 35% tax benefit from itemized deductions, including charitable gifts.
## For 2025
- No non-itemizer deduction and no 0.5% floor; the traditional rules apply (deductible only if you itemize)
## Limits
- Cash contributions to public charities: generally deductible up to 60% of AGI
- Noncash property: generally up to 50% or 30% of AGI depending on the property and organization
- Amounts above the limit generally carry forward up to 5 years
## Substantiation
- Any contribution of $250 or more requires a contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the charity
- Noncash contributions over $500 require Form 8283
- Noncash contributions over $5,000 generally require a qualified appraisal
- Keep bank records or receipts for all cash gifts regardless of amount
## Notes
- Only gifts to QUALIFIED organizations count; gifts to individuals, political campaigns, and most foreign charities do not
- If you receive something in return (dinner, tickets), you may deduct only the amount above the value received
- Volunteer time is never deductible, but out-of-pocket costs and mileage while volunteering are
- Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs) from an IRA bypass the 0.5% floor and are an efficient alternative for those over 70.5
- Bunching (concentrating multiple years of giving into one year) is a common strategy given the high standard deduction
## Questions
- How much did you donate, and were the gifts cash or property?
- Do you have written acknowledgments for gifts of $250 or more?
- Will you itemize, or take the standard deduction (relevant to the new 2026 non-itemizer deduction)?
- What is your AGI (for the 0.5% floor in 2026 and later)?
- Did you donate noncash property over $500 (Form 8283)?
## Common Errors
- Deducting contributions without the required written acknowledgment
- Missing the new 0.5% AGI floor for 2026 itemizers
- Overlooking the new 2026 non-itemizer deduction when taking the standard deduction
- Deducting the full ticket price for charity events instead of only the excess over value received
- Deducting the value of volunteer time
- Donating to a donor-advised fund and claiming the non-itemizer deduction (not eligible)
## Prompt
- I made charitable donations to my church this year.
- I donated to several charities and want to deduct them.
- I take the standard deduction but still donate to charity.
