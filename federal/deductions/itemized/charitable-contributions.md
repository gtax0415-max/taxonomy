# Charitable Contributions
## Description
Donations to qualified charitable organizations. The rules changed significantly for tax year 2026 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, for both itemizers and non-itemizers.
## For 2026 and later: two important changes
1. NON-ITEMIZERS can now deduct cash contributions — up to $1,000 (single) or $2,000 (married filing jointly), under new Section 170(p). This sits on top of the standard deduction. CASH gifts only (clothing and household goods do not qualify), and the recipient must be a Section 170(b)(1)(A) public charity — gifts to DONOR-ADVISED FUNDS, private non-operating foundations, and supporting organizations are excluded. Not indexed for inflation
2. ITEMIZERS face a new 0.5% AGI FLOOR — only contributions exceeding 0.5% of AGI are deductible. Example: AGI $200,000 means the first $1,000 of giving is not deductible
Additionally, taxpayers in the top 37% bracket receive only a 35% tax benefit from itemized deductions, including charitable gifts.
## For 2025
- No non-itemizer deduction and no 0.5% floor; the traditional rules apply (deductible only if you itemize)
## Limits
- Cash contributions to public charities: generally deductible up to 60% of AGI
- Noncash property: generally up to 50% or 30% of AGI depending on the property and organization
- Amounts above the limit generally carry forward up to 5 years
## Substantiation
- Any contribution of $250 or more requires a contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the charity
- Noncash contributions over $500 require Form 8283
- Noncash contributions over $5,000 generally require a qualified appraisal
- Keep bank records or receipts for all cash gifts regardless of amount
## Qualified organizations — IRC Section 170(c)
A contribution is deductible ONLY if it goes to a qualified organization as defined in IRC Section 170(c). Being tax-exempt is not enough — the organization must fall in one of these categories:
- Section 170(c)(2): domestic organizations described in Section 501(c)(3) — religious, charitable, scientific, literary, or educational, and prevention of cruelty to children or animals. This is the main category and covers churches, schools, hospitals, and most public charities. EXCEPTION: 501(c)(3) organizations organized and operated for TESTING FOR PUBLIC SAFETY do not qualify
- Section 170(c)(1): a state, U.S. possession, political subdivision, the United States, or the District of Columbia — but only for gifts made EXCLUSIVELY FOR PUBLIC PURPOSES
- Section 170(c)(3): war veterans organizations (typically Section 501(c)(19))
- Section 170(c)(4): domestic fraternal societies operating under the lodge system (Sections 501(c)(8) and 501(c)(10)) — but ONLY if the gift is used exclusively for religious, charitable, scientific, literary, or educational purposes
- Section 170(c)(5): nonprofit cemetery companies (Section 501(c)(13)) — only for general upkeep, not for the care of a specific plot
## Tax-exempt but NOT deductible
These organizations are exempt from tax yet contributions to them are NOT deductible:
- Section 501(c)(4) social welfare organizations and civic leagues
- Section 501(c)(6) business leagues, chambers of commerce, and trade associations
- Section 501(c)(7) social and recreational clubs
- Political parties, candidates, campaign committees, and political action committees
- Individuals, no matter how needy, including crowdfunding campaigns for a person
- Most foreign organizations (limited treaty exceptions exist for certain Canadian, Mexican, and Israeli charities)
- Homeowners associations
## Verify before claiming
Use the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search (formerly Publication 78) to confirm both that the organization is listed and what its deductibility code is. Do not rely on the organization's own claim that gifts are deductible.
## Notes
- Only gifts to qualified organizations under Section 170(c) count; gifts to individuals, political campaigns, and most foreign charities do not
- Tuition paid to a Section 501(c)(3) school is NOT a charitable contribution — it is a personal expense paid for education services, even though the school is a qualified organization
- Raffle tickets, bingo, and similar chances to win are NOT deductible, because you received full consideration for the payment
- If you receive something in return (dinner, tickets), you may deduct only the amount above the value received
- Volunteer time is never deductible, but out-of-pocket costs and mileage while volunteering are
- Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs) from an IRA bypass the 0.5% floor entirely and are usually better than a deduction for anyone over 70.5, because they reduce AGI rather than taxable income. 2026 limit $111,000 per person — see federal/income/retirement/qualified-charitable-distributions.md
- Bunching (concentrating multiple years of giving into one year) is a common strategy given the high standard deduction
## Questions
- Is the recipient a Section 170(c) qualified organization (most commonly a 501(c)(3))? Did you verify it in the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search?
- How much did you donate, and were the gifts cash or property?
- Do you have written acknowledgments for gifts of $250 or more?
- Will you itemize, or take the standard deduction (relevant to the new 2026 non-itemizer deduction)?
- What is your AGI (for the 0.5% floor in 2026 and later)?
- Did you donate noncash property over $500 (Form 8283)?
## Common Errors
- Deducting contributions without the required written acknowledgment
- Missing the new 0.5% AGI floor for 2026 itemizers
- Overlooking the new 2026 non-itemizer deduction when taking the standard deduction
- Deducting the full ticket price for charity events instead of only the excess over value received
- Deducting the value of volunteer time
- Giving to a tax-exempt organization that is not a Section 170(c) qualified organization (for example a 501(c)(4), 501(c)(6), or 501(c)(7)) and assuming it is deductible
- Deducting school tuition or raffle tickets as charitable gifts
- Donating to a donor-advised fund and claiming the non-itemizer deduction (not eligible)
## Prompt
- I made charitable donations to my church this year.
- I donated to several charities and want to deduct them.
- I take the standard deduction but still donate to charity.
