# Home Mortgage Interest
## Description
Interest paid on a loan secured by your main home or a second home, deducted on Schedule A.
## Box-to-line mapping
- Form 1098 Box 1 (mortgage interest received) → Schedule A Line 8a
- Form 1098 Box 6 (points paid on purchase) → Schedule A Line 8c
- Form 1098 Box 5 (mortgage insurance premiums) → deductible for 2026 and later, subject to an AGI phase-out
- Form 1098 Box 10 may report real estate taxes paid from escrow → Schedule A Line 5b (see real-estate-taxes.md)
## Notes
- Deductible only if you itemize
- Interest is deductible on up to $750,000 of acquisition debt ($375,000 married filing separately) for loans taken after December 15, 2017. Loans before that date are grandfathered at $1,000,000. The $750,000 limit was made PERMANENT by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act
- The loan must be SECURED by the home and used to buy, build, or substantially improve it
- Home equity loan interest is deductible ONLY if the funds were used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home securing the loan — not for personal expenses like paying off credit cards
- Applies to a main home and one second home
- Points paid on a home purchase are generally deductible in the year paid; points on a refinance must usually be spread over the life of the loan
- Beginning in 2026, mortgage insurance premiums are treated as deductible mortgage interest, phasing out as AGI rises
## Required Information
- Form 1098 from each lender (Box 1 interest, Box 6 points)
- Loan origination date and original balance (for the debt limit)
- How home equity loan proceeds were used
## Questions
- Do you own a home with a mortgage, and did you receive a Form 1098?
- When was the loan taken out (before or after December 15, 2017)?
- Is the loan balance above $750,000?
- Do you have a home equity loan, and what were the funds used for?
- Did you refinance or pay points this year?
## Common Errors
- Deducting home equity interest used for personal expenses
- Ignoring the $750,000 acquisition debt limit on large mortgages
- Deducting the full principal payment instead of only interest
- Deducting refinance points all at once instead of amortizing them
- Missing real estate taxes reported on the same Form 1098 (those go on Schedule A Line 5b)
## Prompt
- I own a home and paid mortgage interest this year.
- I received a Form 1098 from my lender.
- I refinanced my mortgage this year.
