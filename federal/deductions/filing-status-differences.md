# Filing Status Differences Across Deductions
## Description
Reference showing which deductions change with filing status. Some caps double for married couples filing jointly, some do NOT (creating a marriage penalty), and several are barred entirely for married filing separately.
## Barred entirely for Married Filing Separately (MFS)
These are not merely reduced — MFS filers cannot claim them at all:
- Student loan interest deduction (adjustments/student-loan-interest.md)
- Qualified tips deduction (schedule-1-a/qualified-tips.md) — married taxpayers must file jointly
- Qualified overtime deduction (schedule-1-a/qualified-overtime.md) — married taxpayers must file jointly
- Enhanced senior deduction (schedule-1-a/senior-deduction.md) — married taxpayers must file jointly
- IRA deduction is effectively eliminated for MFS filers covered by a workplace plan (phase-out range is only $0-$10,000)
## NOT doubled for joint filers — marriage penalties
The single-filer amount is the same as the married-filing-jointly amount, so two unmarried people get twice what one married couple gets:
- SALT cap: $40,400 for 2026 whether single or MFJ (itemized/salt.md). Two singles = $80,800 combined; a married couple = $40,400
- SALT phase-down threshold: $505,000 for 2026 for both single and MFJ — also not doubled
- Qualified tips: $25,000 per return regardless of status (schedule-1-a/qualified-tips.md). A couple where both spouses earn tips still shares one $25,000 cap
- Car loan interest: $10,000 per return regardless of status (schedule-1-a/car-loan-interest.md). Single and joint filers share the same cap
## Doubled (or effectively doubled) for joint filers
- Standard deduction: $16,100 single / $32,200 MFJ for 2026 (standard/standard-deduction.md)
- Qualified overtime: $12,500 single / $25,000 MFJ (schedule-1-a/qualified-overtime.md)
- Enhanced senior deduction: $6,000 per person / $12,000 if both spouses qualify (schedule-1-a/senior-deduction.md)
- Car loan interest MAGI phase-out: $100,000 single / $200,000 MFJ — the threshold IS doubled even though the cap is not
- Senior deduction MAGI phase-out: $75,000 single / $150,000 MFJ
- Charitable non-itemizer deduction (2026): $1,000 single / $2,000 MFJ (itemized/charitable-contributions.md)
- QBI income threshold: roughly doubled for MFJ (qbi/qbi.md)
- Tips and overtime MAGI phase-out: $150,000 single / $300,000 MFJ
## Halved for Married Filing Separately
- SALT cap: half the full cap ($20,200 for 2026)
- SALT phase-down threshold: half ($252,500 for 2026)
- Mortgage acquisition debt limit: $750,000 becomes $375,000 (itemized/mortgage-interest.md)
## Where MFS can actually HELP
- Medical expenses (itemized/medical-expenses.md): the 7.5% floor applies to each spouse's own AGI, so a spouse with high medical costs and low income clears the floor more easily than against combined AGI. This is the main legitimate reason to consider MFS, but weigh it against everything MFS costs
## Different phase-out ranges by status (not simple doubling)
- Student loan interest, 2026: single/HOH/QSS $85,000-$100,000; MFJ $175,000-$205,000 (a $15,000 window vs a $30,000 window)
- IRA deduction, 2026: single/HOH $81,000-$91,000; MFJ when you are covered $129,000-$149,000; MFJ when only your spouse is covered $242,000-$252,000; MFS $0-$10,000
## No filing status difference
These depend on other factors rather than filing status:
- HSA deduction — depends on coverage type (self-only vs family), not filing status
- Medical expense floor rate — 7.5% for everyone (though the AGI it applies to differs)
- Casualty losses — $100 per event and 10% of AGI for everyone
- Investment interest — limited by net investment income
- Deductible part of self-employment tax — based on self-employment earnings
- SEP, SIMPLE, and qualified plan contributions — based on self-employment earnings
- Business expenses (Schedule C) — based on the business, not the return
- Educator expenses — per eligible educator, so a couple where both qualify gets double
## The MFS trap
If one spouse itemizes, the other MUST itemize too — the second spouse cannot take the standard deduction (standard/standard-deduction.md). Combined with the barred deductions above, MFS is usually costly and should be modeled both ways before filing.
## Questions
- What is your filing status?
- If married, have you compared filing jointly versus separately?
- Do you have large medical expenses relative to one spouse's income (the main MFS advantage)?
- Are you claiming tips, overtime, senior, or student loan interest deductions (all of which require joint filing)?
## Prompt
- Should we file jointly or separately?
- How does my filing status change what I can deduct?
