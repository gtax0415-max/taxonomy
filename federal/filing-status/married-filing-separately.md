# Married Filing Separately (MFS)
## Description
Two separate returns for a married couple, each reporting only their own income and deductions. Almost always produces a higher combined tax, but a handful of situations justify it.
## 2026 amounts
- Standard deduction: $16,100, the same as single
- Additional standard deduction for age 65 or older, or blindness: $1,650 per condition
## What you LOSE by filing separately
This status is barred from, not merely reduced for:
- EARNED INCOME CREDIT
- EDUCATION CREDITS — American Opportunity and Lifetime Learning
- STUDENT LOAN INTEREST deduction
- QUALIFIED TIPS, QUALIFIED OVERTIME, and the SENIOR DEDUCTION on Schedule 1-A, which all require a joint return if married
- CHILD AND DEPENDENT CARE CREDIT, except for spouses living apart
And these are halved:
- SALT cap: $20,200 for 2026, with the phase-down threshold also halved to $252,500
- Mortgage acquisition debt limit: $375,000 instead of $750,000
- Capital loss against ordinary income: $1,500 instead of $3,000
- Rental passive loss allowance: $12,500, and only if you lived apart from your spouse for the ENTIRE year. If you lived together at any point it is $0
The IRA deduction is effectively eliminated for an MFS filer covered by a workplace plan — the phase-out range is only $0 to $10,000.
## The itemizing trap
If one spouse itemizes, the other MUST itemize too. The second spouse cannot take the standard deduction, and if they have few itemized deductions their taxable income can rise sharply. Coordinate before either return is filed.
## When separate filing can still win
- LARGE MEDICAL EXPENSES for one spouse. The 7.5% AGI floor is applied to each spouse's own AGI, so a spouse with high bills and low income clears the floor more easily than against the couple's combined AGI
- INCOME-DRIVEN STUDENT LOAN REPAYMENT plans that base the payment on the borrower's own AGI. The tax cost can be smaller than the payment increase
- LIABILITY PROTECTION. Separate returns avoid joint and several liability where one spouse has questionable income or unpaid obligations
- INJURED SPOUSE situations where a refund would be seized for the other spouse's debts, although Form 8379 is often the better tool
## Community property states
In Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin, most income earned during marriage must be split 50-50 between the two separate returns regardless of who earned it. This removes much of the benefit and adds significant complexity.
## Notes
- You can amend from separate to joint within three years. You CANNOT amend from joint to separate after the filing deadline
- Always model both ways before filing. The lost credits frequently exceed the separation benefit
## Questions
- Does one spouse have large medical expenses relative to their own income?
- Is either spouse on an income-driven student loan repayment plan?
- Is there a liability concern about the other spouse's tax positions?
- Do you live in a community property state?
- Have you modeled the combined tax both ways?
## Common Errors
- Filing separately without modeling joint, and losing more in credits than the separation saves
- One spouse itemizing while the other takes the standard deduction
- Claiming the rental passive loss allowance after living with the spouse during the year
- Overlooking community property income-splitting rules
- Attempting to switch to joint after the deadline for a year already filed separately
## Prompt
- Should my spouse and I file separately?
- My spouse has a lot of medical bills this year.
- I am on an income-based student loan plan.
- I do not want to be responsible for my spouse's taxes.
