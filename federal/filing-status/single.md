# Single
## Description
The default status for an unmarried taxpayer who does not qualify for head of household or qualifying surviving spouse.
## Who files as single
On DECEMBER 31 of the tax year you were:
- Never married, or
- Divorced under a final decree, or
- Legally separated under a decree of divorce or separate maintenance
State law determines whether a separation decree counts. Simply living apart is NOT enough — without a decree you are still married for federal tax purposes.
## 2026 amounts
- Standard deduction: $16,100
- Additional standard deduction for age 65 or older, or blindness: $2,050 per condition
## Check head of household first
Many unmarried taxpayers with a child or dependent parent default to single when they qualify for HEAD OF HOUSEHOLD. For 2026 that mistake costs $8,050 of standard deduction ($24,150 versus $16,100), plus wider brackets. See head-of-household.md before choosing single.
## Notes
- A widow or widower whose spouse died within the last two years and who has a dependent child should check qualifying-surviving-spouse.md first
- In the YEAR a spouse dies, you generally file jointly, not single
## Questions
- Were you unmarried, divorced, or legally separated on December 31?
- Do you have a child, parent, or other relative you support who lives with you?
- Did your spouse die within the last three years?
## Common Errors
- Filing single when head of household applies
- Filing single in the year a spouse died, when a joint return is generally available
- Assuming physical separation makes you unmarried without a legal decree
## Prompt
- I am not married and file my own taxes.
- I got divorced this year.
- I live alone and have no dependents.
