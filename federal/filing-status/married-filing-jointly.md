# Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
## Description
One return covering both spouses, combining all income, deductions, and credits. The most common status for married couples and usually the lowest total tax.
## Who can file jointly
- Married on DECEMBER 31, even if married on that single day
- Living apart does not prevent a joint return, as long as you are not legally separated under a decree
- If your SPOUSE DIED during the year and you did not remarry, you may still file jointly for that year
- Both spouses must SIGN, and both become JOINTLY AND SEVERALLY LIABLE for the entire tax
## 2026 amounts
- Standard deduction: $32,200 — exactly double the single amount, so the standard deduction itself is marriage-neutral
- Additional standard deduction for age 65 or older, or blindness: $1,650 per condition, per spouse. A couple both 65 or older adds $3,300
## Why it is usually better
- Widest tax brackets at every rate
- Access to credits that married filing separately loses entirely: the Earned Income Credit, education credits, and the Schedule 1-A tips, overtime, and senior deductions
- Higher phase-out thresholds for most credits and deductions
## Where joint filing does NOT double the benefit
Several thresholds are NOT doubled, which produces marriage penalties:
- SALT cap: $40,400 for 2026 whether single or joint, and the $505,000 phase-down threshold is also shared
- Qualified tips deduction: $25,000 per return, not per spouse
- Car loan interest deduction: $10,000 per return
- Capital loss against ordinary income: $3,000 either way
- Rental passive loss $25,000 special allowance and its $100,000 phase-out
- Adoption credit phase-out range
See federal/deductions/filing-status-differences.md for the full list.
## Joint and several liability
Both spouses are responsible for the ENTIRE tax, interest, and penalties, regardless of who earned the income. This survives divorce. Relief exists — innocent spouse relief, separation of liability, and equitable relief on Form 8857 — but it is not automatic and is often denied.
## Notes
- You can amend from separate to joint within three years, but NOT from joint to separate after the filing deadline
- If one spouse is a nonresident alien, a joint return is possible only by electing to treat them as a resident, which subjects their worldwide income to U.S. tax
## Questions
- Were you married on December 31?
- Did your spouse die during the year?
- Have you compared joint versus separate, especially if one spouse has large medical expenses or student loan payments?
- Are you comfortable with joint and several liability for your spouse's tax positions?
## Common Errors
- Assuming joint is always better without modeling separate in edge cases
- Overlooking joint and several liability when a spouse has unreported income
- Trying to switch from joint to separate after the deadline, which is not allowed
- Assuming every threshold doubles for joint filers
## Prompt
- We are married and file together.
- I got married this year.
- My spouse passed away this year and I am filing our taxes.
