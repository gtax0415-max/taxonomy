# Head of Household (HOH)
## Description
A status for an unmarried taxpayer who maintains a home for a qualifying person. It sits between single and married filing jointly, and for 2026 it is worth $8,050 more in standard deduction than single, plus wider brackets.
## Three tests — all must be met
1. UNMARRIED, or "considered unmarried," on December 31
2. Paid MORE THAN HALF the cost of keeping up your home for the year
3. A QUALIFYING PERSON lived with you more than half the year
Failing any one costs the entire status.
## Test 1: unmarried or considered unmarried
- Never married, divorced under a final decree, or legally separated under a decree of divorce or separate maintenance as of December 31
- Living in separate homes is NOT enough by itself. State law governs whether a separation decree exists
- CONSIDERED UNMARRIED: a married taxpayer can still qualify if they lived APART from their spouse for the ENTIRE LAST SIX MONTHS of the year, file a separate return, paid more than half the cost of the home, and their DEPENDENT CHILD lived in that home more than half the year. This is the only path by which a married person reaches head of household
## Test 2: more than half the cost of keeping up the home
Counts: rent or mortgage interest, property taxes, home insurance, repairs, utilities, and food eaten in the home.
Does NOT count: clothing, education, medical care, vacations, life insurance, transportation, or the value of your own services.
Keep records — this is a frequently examined item.
## Test 3: a qualifying person
- A QUALIFYING CHILD who lived with you more than half the year
- A DEPENDENT PARENT, who does NOT have to live with you as long as you paid more than half the cost of their main home, including a nursing home
- Certain other DEPENDENT RELATIVES who lived with you more than half the year
A qualifying person must generally be your dependent, with a narrow exception for a child released to the other parent on Form 8332 — releasing the dependency exemption does NOT transfer head of household status. The custodial parent keeps it.
## 2026 amounts
- Standard deduction: $24,150, versus $16,100 for single and $32,200 for joint
- Additional standard deduction for age 65 or older, or blindness: $2,050 per condition
## Notes
- Temporary absences for school, illness, business, vacation, or military service still count as living with you
- A noncustodial parent cannot claim head of household even with Form 8332
- If your spouse died within the last two years and you have a dependent child, check qualifying-surviving-spouse.md first — it carries the larger joint standard deduction
## Questions
- Were you unmarried or considered unmarried on December 31?
- Did you pay more than half the cost of keeping up your home?
- Who lived with you, and for how many months?
- If married, did you live apart from your spouse for the entire last six months with a dependent child in the home?
- Do you support a parent who lives elsewhere?
## Common Errors
- Filing single by default when head of household applies
- Assuming that living apart from a spouse is enough without meeting the full considered-unmarried test
- A noncustodial parent claiming head of household after receiving Form 8332
- Both parents claiming head of household for the same child
- Counting clothing, medical care, or education toward the cost of keeping up a home
- Missing that a dependent parent qualifies without living with you
## Prompt
- I am a single parent with two kids.
- I support my mother who lives in a nursing home.
- I am separated from my spouse and my child lives with me.
