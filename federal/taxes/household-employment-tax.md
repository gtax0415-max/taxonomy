# Household Employment Taxes (Schedule H)
## Description
Social Security, Medicare, and federal unemployment tax owed when you employ someone in your home — commonly called the "nanny tax." It covers nannies, housekeepers, home health aides, private caregivers, gardeners, and drivers.
## When it applies
- CASH WAGES of $3,000 or more to any ONE household employee during 2026 triggers Social Security and Medicare tax (IRS Publication 926, 2026 edition). This is up from $2,800 for 2025 and $2,700 for 2024. The Social Security Administration resets it each October based on the national average wage index
- The threshold is PER EMPLOYEE, PER YEAR. Paying one sitter $2,000 and another $2,000 triggers nothing. Paying one nanny $3,000 makes ALL $3,000 taxable, not just the amount above the threshold
- $1,000 or more in ANY CALENDAR QUARTER to all household employees COMBINED triggers federal unemployment tax (FUTA). This threshold did NOT change for 2026
- The two tests are independent. You can owe FUTA without owing Social Security and Medicare, or the reverse. Filing Schedule H with the FICA columns complete and the FUTA column blank is a pattern the IRS flags
## Schedule H line-by-line
PART I — Social Security, Medicare, and federal income tax
- Line A, B, C: the threshold questions. Answering all three "No" means you do not file the schedule at all
- Line 1a: total cash wages subject to Social Security tax
- Line 2: Social Security tax, Line 1a x 12.4%
- Line 3: total cash wages subject to Medicare tax
- Line 4: Medicare tax, Line 3 x 2.9%
- Line 5: cash wages subject to Additional Medicare Tax withholding
- Line 6: Additional Medicare Tax withholding, Line 5 x 0.9%
- Line 7: federal income tax withheld, if you agreed to withhold any
- Line 8: TOTAL of Lines 2, 4, 6, and 7
- Line 9: the $1,000-per-quarter FUTA question. Answering "No" stops the schedule here and sends Line 8 straight to Schedule 2
PART II — FUTA. Section A for a single state, Section B if you paid into more than one state fund
- Line 15: total cash wages subject to FUTA tax
- Line 16: FUTA tax at the effective rate
- Section B, Lines 20 through 24: the 6.0% gross tax less the credit for state contributions, capped at 5.4%
PART III — Total
- Line 25: the amount from Line 8
- Line 26: Line 25 plus Line 16 or Line 24. THIS is the number that carries to Schedule 2
PART IV — address and signature, required only if you are NOT filing Form 1040
## Where Line 26 goes on Schedule 2 — changing for 2026
Through 2025, Schedule H Line 26 (or Line 8 if you stopped early) went to SCHEDULE 2, LINE 9. The IRS DRAFT 2026 Schedule H directs it instead to SCHEDULE 2, LINE 17a. Draft forms are not final, and secondary sources still show Line 9, so verify against the released 2026 Schedule H and Schedule 2 before filing. Either way it lands on Form 1040 Line 23.
## What is NOT a source document
- FORM W-2 and FORM W-3 are forms you CREATE and ISSUE. They report the same wages to the employee and the Social Security Administration, but they are outputs of your payroll records, not inputs to Schedule H
- FORM I-9 is an employment eligibility record with no tax figures at all. Keep it, but it feeds no line of any return
- The real sources are your own PAYROLL RECORDS and your STATE UNEMPLOYMENT PAYMENT records. The state contributions matter directly: they generate the FUTA credit that drops the 6.0% rate to 0.6%
## Employee or independent contractor
This is the threshold question and it is not yours to choose freely. If you control WHAT work is done and HOW it is done, the worker is your EMPLOYEE, regardless of what you agree to call them or how often they are paid. A nanny who works in your home on your schedule with your instructions is an employee. A gardener who brings their own equipment, sets their own hours, and serves many clients is generally an independent contractor.
Misclassifying an employee as a contractor is the most common and most expensive error here.
## What you owe
- Social Security and Medicare: 15.3% total, split 7.65% employee and 7.65% employer. You may pay the employee's share yourself, but if you do, that amount becomes additional taxable wages to them
- FUTA: 6% on the first $7,000 of wages, usually reduced to 0.6% by the credit for state unemployment taxes paid
- Federal income tax withholding is NOT required for household employees, but you may do it if the employee asks on a Form W-4
## What else is required
- Get an EIN. You cannot use your Social Security number as the employer
- Issue Form W-2 to the employee and file Form W-3 with the Social Security Administration by January 31. For 2026 wages that deadline is FEBRUARY 1, 2027, because January 31 falls on a Sunday
- Form W-2 Box 3 caps at the 2026 Social Security wage base of $184,500; Box 5 (Medicare) has no cap
- If the employee worked qualifying overtime, Form W-2 Box 12 code TT applies to household employers too — see federal/deductions/schedule-1-a/qualified-overtime.md
- Verify work eligibility on Form I-9 at hiring
- Register with your state for unemployment insurance, which usually has a LOWER threshold than the federal one
- Pay through quarterly estimated tax or extra withholding from your own job, since Schedule H tax is not withheld
## Who is exempt
Wages paid to your spouse, your child under 21, your parent (with exceptions), or an employee under 18 whose principal occupation is not household work — a student babysitter, for example.
## Related
- Paying for care so you can work may also produce a credit: federal/credits/family-dependent/child-dependent-care-credit.md. Claiming that credit requires reporting the provider's TIN, which is how many household employer situations come to light. A Form 2441 showing large childcare wages with no Schedule H attached is a common mismatch
- A dependent care FSA can cover nanny wages pre-tax. The 2026 limit rose to $7,500 for joint filers and $3,750 for married filing separately, up from $5,000 and $2,500
## Required Information
- Total cash wages paid to each household employee
- Wages by calendar quarter, for the FUTA test
- Your EIN
- State unemployment taxes paid
- Copies of Forms W-2 and W-3 filed, as evidence that the reporting obligation was met
- Pay dates, so quarterly totals can be tested against the $1,000 FUTA threshold
## Questions
- Did you pay anyone to work in your home — a nanny, housekeeper, caregiver, or driver?
- How much did you pay each person during the year?
- Did total household wages reach $1,000 in any quarter?
- Do you control how and when the work is done (employee) or does the worker (contractor)?
- Do you have an EIN and did you issue a Form W-2?
## Common Errors
- Classifying a nanny as an independent contractor and issuing a Form 1099
- Missing the state unemployment registration, which often has a lower threshold than federal
- Failing to issue Form W-2 by the deadline, which is February 1, 2027 for 2026 wages
- Paying state unemployment contributions LATE, which reduces or forfeits the FUTA credit and leaves the rate above 0.6%
- Not paying quarterly estimates and facing an underpayment penalty at filing
- Claiming the child and dependent care credit with a provider TIN while never filing Schedule H, which flags the mismatch
## Prompt
- I pay a nanny to watch my kids.
- I have a housekeeper who comes weekly.
- Do I owe nanny tax?
