# Additional Taxes on IRAs and Tax-Favored Accounts (Form 5329)
## Description
Penalty taxes on retirement and other tax-favored accounts — early withdrawals, missed required distributions, and excess contributions. Filed on about 2.7 million returns (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## You can often SKIP Form 5329 entirely
Schedule 2 Line 8 has a CHECKBOX for exactly this. You do NOT have to file Form 5329 if BOTH are true:
1. Distribution code 1 is CORRECTLY shown in Form 1099-R Box 7a on EVERY Form 1099-R you received, AND
2. You owe the 10% on the FULL taxable amount of each one — no exception applies to any part
In that case, multiply the taxable amount by 10%, enter the result on Schedule 2 Line 8, and CHECK THE BOX indicating you are not liable to file Form 5329. Nothing else is required.
## When Form 5329 IS required
Any one of these forces the form:
- Code 1 is NOT shown, or is shown INCORRECTLY, in Form 1099-R Box 7a
- An exception applies but Box 7a does not identify it
- An exception applies to only PART of the distribution. Partial exceptions always require the form — you cannot check the box and reduce the amount
- You owe any OTHER Form 5329 tax: a missed required minimum distribution, an excess contribution, a Coverdell, QTP, or ABLE distribution, or a Roth IRA distribution with a recapture amount
- You are requesting a WAIVER of the RMD excise tax for reasonable cause
The checkbox shortcut covers ONLY the 10% early distribution tax in its simplest form. The RMD excise tax and the excess contribution tax ALWAYS require Form 5329.
On a joint return where both spouses owe a Form 5329 tax, each completes a SEPARATE Form 5329, and the combined tax goes on the single Schedule 2 Line 8.
## The three main penalties
### 10% early distribution tax
- Applies to distributions before age 59.5 from IRAs, 401(k)s, and similar plans
- The Form 1099-R Box 7a distribution CODE tells you whether an exception already applies. Code 1 means no known exception; code 2 means an exception applies
- Exceptions include: death, total and permanent disability, qualified higher education expenses (IRAs only), first-time home purchase up to $10,000 lifetime (IRAs only), unreimbursed medical expenses above 7.5% of AGI, a series of substantially equal periodic payments, birth or adoption up to $5,000, qualified disaster recovery, terminal illness, domestic abuse, and separation from service at age 55 or later (employer plans only)
- If the payer coded the form incorrectly, you claim the exception on Form 5329 with the appropriate exception code
- Form 5329 exception codes are entered on its Line 2. Common ones: 01 separation from service at age 55 or later (not IRAs), 02 substantially equal periodic payments, 03 total and permanent disability, 04 death, 05 medical expenses deductible on Schedule A
### 25% excise tax on a missed required minimum distribution
- SECURE 2.0 reduced this from 50% to 25%
- It drops further to 10% if corrected within a two-year correction window
- RMD start age depends on birth year: 72 if born 1949 or earlier, 73 if born 1950 through 1959, 75 if born 1960 or later
- The first RMD may be delayed to April 1 of the following year, but that stacks two RMDs into one tax year
- A waiver for reasonable cause can be requested on Form 5329
- Roth IRAs have no RMD during the owner's lifetime; Roth 401(k)s lost their RMD requirement beginning 2024
### 6% excess contribution tax
- Applies to contributions above the annual limit to an IRA, Roth IRA, HSA, or Coverdell account
- The 6% recurs EVERY YEAR the excess remains in the account, so it compounds if ignored
- Correcting before the filing deadline, including extensions, avoids it — withdraw the excess plus the earnings attributable to it
## Other taxes on this form
- 20% additional tax on non-qualified HSA distributions used for anything other than medical care
- Additional tax on non-qualified distributions from ABLE and Coverdell accounts

## Required Information
- Form 1099-R for each distribution, with the Box 7a code
- Your age at the date of each distribution
- Prior-year December 31 account balances and the applicable RMD divisor
- Contribution amounts against the annual limit
- Documentation for any exception claimed
## Questions
- Did you take a retirement distribution before age 59.5?
- What is the Form 1099-R Box 7a code, and does an exception apply?
- Are you past your RMD start age, and did you take the full amount by December 31?
- Did you contribute more than the limit to an IRA, Roth IRA, or HSA?
- Did you use HSA funds for something other than medical care?
- Does the Form 1099-R show code 1 on the full amount, so you can check the Schedule 2 Line 8 box and skip Form 5329?
## Common Errors
- Accepting a Box 7a code 1 without checking whether an exception applies
- Filing Form 5329 unnecessarily when code 1 is correct on the full amount and the Schedule 2 Line 8 checkbox would do
- Checking the Schedule 2 Line 8 box when an exception applies to only PART of the distribution, which requires the form
- Assuming the checkbox shortcut covers a missed RMD or an excess contribution — it does not
- Missing an RMD and not filing Form 5329 to request a waiver
- Leaving an excess contribution in the account, which re-triggers the 6% every year
- Assuming the first-time homebuyer and education exceptions apply to 401(k) plans — they are IRA-only
- Forgetting that the age-55 separation-from-service exception does NOT apply to IRAs
## Prompt
- I took money out of my 401(k) early.
- I forgot to take my required minimum distribution.
- I contributed too much to my IRA.
- I used my HSA for something that was not a medical expense.
