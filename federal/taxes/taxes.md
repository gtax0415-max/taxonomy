# Additional Taxes (Schedule 2)
## Description
Taxes that are NOT the ordinary income tax computed on Form 1040 Line 16. They are calculated on their own forms, collected on Schedule 2, and added to the return. Schedule 2 appears on about 38.5 million returns (IRS SOI, TY 2022), the sixth most-filed schedule.
## Why this category exists
Credits reduce the income tax on Line 16. Most of them do NOT touch the taxes on Schedule 2. A self-employed filer with enough credits to zero out income tax still owes the full self-employment tax. Understanding which bucket a tax sits in determines whether a credit can offset it.

## The two thresholds to memorize
The Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%) and the net investment income tax (3.8%) share the same thresholds, and NEITHER is indexed for inflation — they have been frozen since 2013:
| Filing status | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Single / head of household | $200,000 |
| Married filing jointly / QSS | $250,000 |
| Married filing separately | $125,000 |
Because they never move, each year more taxpayers cross them.
## Questions
- Are you self-employed or a partner in a partnership?
- Is your wage or self-employment income above $200,000 ($250,000 joint)?
- Do you have investment income and MAGI above those same thresholds?
- Did you exercise incentive stock options, or claim large state and local tax deductions?
- Did you take an early retirement distribution, miss an RMD, or over-contribute to an account?
- Did you pay a nanny, housekeeper, or home health aide?
## Prompt
- Why do I owe more tax than my bracket suggests?
- I have no income tax due but still owe money.
