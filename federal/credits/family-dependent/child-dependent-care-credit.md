# Child and Dependent Care Credit
## Description
Nonrefundable credit for care expenses paid so you (and your spouse, if married) could work or look for work. Claimed on Form 2441, filed on about 6.5 million returns (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## Amounts for 2026 — expanded by OBBBA
- Eligible expenses are capped at $3,000 for ONE qualifying person or $6,000 for TWO OR MORE
- The credit is a PERCENTAGE of those expenses, based on AGI. Beginning in 2026 the top rate rose from 35% to 50%
- 2026 tiers: 50% for AGI of $15,000 or less, phasing down to 35%, then to a floor of 20% at higher income. The 20% floor is reached around $105,000 (single) or $210,000 (married filing jointly)
- 2026 maximum credit: $1,500 for one qualifying person (50% of $3,000) or $3,000 for two or more (50% of $6,000)
- For comparison, the 2025 top rate was 35%, giving a maximum of $1,050 or $2,100
- NONREFUNDABLE — it can reduce tax to zero but produces no refund on its own
## Qualifying person
- A child under 13 when the care was provided, or
- A spouse or dependent of any age who is physically or mentally incapable of self-care and lived with you more than half the year
## Key requirements
- You (and your spouse if married) must have EARNED INCOME. The credit is limited to the lower spouse's earned income, so a married couple where one spouse has no earned income generally cannot claim it. A full-time student or disabled spouse is treated as having a modest amount of earned income
- Married taxpayers generally must FILE JOINTLY, with an exception for those living apart
- You must report the care provider's NAME, ADDRESS, and TIN or SSN on Form 2441. Missing provider information is a common reason the credit is denied
- The care must let you WORK or LOOK FOR WORK — not simply to have free time
## Interaction with a dependent care FSA
Dependent care FSA contributions reduce the expenses eligible for this credit DOLLAR FOR DOLLAR. Employer-provided dependent care benefits appear in Form W-2 Box 10. Beginning in 2026 the FSA limit rose from $5,000 to $7,500, which for many families uses up the expense cap entirely. Run both options before choosing.
## What does NOT qualify
- Overnight camp (day camp does qualify)
- School tuition at kindergarten level or above (before-school and after-school care does qualify)
- Care provided by your spouse, by the child's parent, by your own child under 19, or by anyone you claim as a dependent
## Required Information
- Care provider name, address, and TIN or SSN
- Amount paid to each provider
- Each qualifying person's name and SSN
- Earned income for you and your spouse
- Form W-2 Box 10 dependent care benefits, if any
## Questions
- Did you pay for care for a child under 13 or a dependent unable to care for themselves?
- Was the care needed so you (and your spouse) could work or look for work?
- Do you have the provider's TIN or SSN?
- Did you or your spouse have earned income all year?
- Did you use a dependent care FSA (Form W-2 Box 10)?
## Common Errors
- Missing the provider's TIN, which can cost the entire credit
- Claiming overnight camp or school tuition
- Claiming it when one spouse had no earned income
- Double-counting expenses already reimbursed through a dependent care FSA
- Using the 2025 35% rate for a 2026 return
## Prompt
- I paid for daycare so I could work.
- We have an after-school program for our son.
- I pay someone to care for my disabled spouse during the day.
