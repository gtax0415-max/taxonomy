# Child Tax Credit and Credit for Other Dependents
## Description
Credit for each qualifying child under 17, plus a smaller credit for other dependents who do not qualify for the main credit. Claimed on Schedule 8812, the third most-filed schedule (~47.4 million returns, IRS SOI TY 2022).
## Amounts
- CHILD TAX CREDIT: $2,200 per qualifying child under age 17 at the end of the year. Made permanent by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act and indexed for inflation beginning 2026
- ADDITIONAL CHILD TAX CREDIT (the refundable part): up to $1,700 per child, also indexed beginning 2026
- CREDIT FOR OTHER DEPENDENTS: $500 per dependent who does not qualify for the Child Tax Credit — a child 17 or older, a college student, an elderly parent. Permanent. NEVER refundable
## Phase-out
- Reduced by $50 for every $1,000 (or fraction) of modified AGI above $200,000 (single, head of household, married filing separately) or $400,000 (married filing jointly)
- These thresholds were made PERMANENT by OBBBA. The same thresholds apply to the Credit for Other Dependents
- The thresholds are NOT doubled for joint filers in the usual sense — $400,000 is exactly double $200,000, so this one is symmetric
## Social Security number requirement — a significant change
Beginning in 2025, a WORK-ELIGIBLE SSN is required for the taxpayer, for the spouse on a joint return, AND for each qualifying child. An ITIN-only filer can no longer claim the Child Tax Credit. This disqualifies many mixed-status households where the child is a U.S. citizen with an SSN but a parent files with an ITIN. The Credit for Other Dependents has a less strict identification requirement.
## Qualifying child tests
- Age: under 17 at the end of the tax year
- Relationship: son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or a descendant of any of these
- Residency: lived with you more than half the year
- Support: the child did not provide more than half of their own support
- Citizenship: U.S. citizen, national, or resident alien
- Not filing a joint return (with limited exceptions)
## Notes
- Under the pre-OBBBA sunset, this would have dropped to $1,000 per child in 2026 with far lower phase-out thresholds. OBBBA prevented that
- The refundable Additional Child Tax Credit is calculated as 15% of earned income above $2,500, capped at $1,700 per child, so families with very little earned income may get less than the full refundable amount
## Required Information
- Each dependent's name, SSN, and date of birth
- Relationship to you and months lived with you
- Your modified AGI and filing status
- Whether you and your spouse have work-eligible SSNs
## Questions
- How many children under 17 do you have, and do they have work-eligible SSNs?
- Do you have other dependents who are 17 or older, or a dependent parent?
- Did each child live with you more than half the year?
- Is your modified AGI above $200,000 ($400,000 joint)?
- Do you and your spouse have work-eligible SSNs?
## Common Errors
- Claiming a child who turned 17 during the year (the age test is under 17 at year end)
- Missing the $500 Credit for Other Dependents for a college-age child or dependent parent
- Claiming the credit with an ITIN after the 2025 SSN requirement took effect
- Two taxpayers claiming the same child (tiebreaker rules apply)
- Expecting the full $1,700 refundable amount with little or no earned income
## Prompt
- I have two kids and want to claim the child tax credit.
- My daughter turned 17 this year.
- I support my elderly mother who lives with me.
