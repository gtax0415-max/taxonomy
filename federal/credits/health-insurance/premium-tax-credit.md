# Premium Tax Credit (Form 8962)
## Description
Refundable credit that subsidizes health insurance bought through the Health Insurance Marketplace. Form 8962 appears on about 9.0 million returns (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## Big change for 2026: the enhancement expired
The temporary enhancement in place from 2021 through 2025 ended December 31, 2025. The credit itself continues — it was never scheduled to sunset — but the enhanced terms did. Three things revert for 2026:
1. THE 400% CLIFF RETURNS. Households above 400% of the federal poverty level lose the credit ENTIRELY. This is a cliff, not a phase-out: one extra dollar of income can cost thousands
2. SUBSIDIES SHRINK. The required contribution percentages rise, so the same household pays more. Coverage that was free at 100-150% of FPL is no longer free
3. FULL REPAYMENT OF EXCESS ADVANCE PAYMENTS. The caps that limited how much excess advance credit had to be repaid are gone. If you underestimated income, you repay the entire excess
## How it works
- The credit is the difference between the benchmark plan premium (the second lowest cost silver plan) and your required contribution based on household income as a percentage of the federal poverty level
- Most people take it as ADVANCE payments (APTC) paid directly to the insurer each month, then reconcile on Form 8962 at filing
- Reconciliation goes both ways: too little advance credit produces a refundable credit on Form 1040 Line 31; too much produces an additional TAX on Schedule 2
## Eligibility
- Enrolled in a Marketplace plan
- Household income generally between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level for 2026
- NOT eligible for affordable employer coverage or for government coverage such as Medicare or Medicaid
- Cannot file MARRIED FILING SEPARATELY, with exceptions for domestic abuse and spousal abandonment
- Cannot be claimed as a dependent by someone else
## Form 1095-A is required
You cannot complete Form 8962 without Form 1095-A from the Marketplace. Filing without reconciling advance payments will stall the return and can block future advance payments.
## Notes
- Income for this purpose is household MAGI, which INCLUDES tax-exempt interest and the nontaxable portion of Social Security benefits
- Because the cliff is based on year-end income, a late Roth conversion, capital gain, or bonus can trigger full repayment of a year's subsidies
- The self-employed health insurance deduction interacts with this credit and requires an iterative calculation
## Required Information
- Form 1095-A for every Marketplace policy, all three columns by month
- Household size and everyone's MAGI
- Months of Marketplace coverage
- Whether affordable employer coverage was available in any month
## Questions
- Did you have Marketplace coverage, and do you have Form 1095-A?
- Did you take advance payments during the year?
- What is your household income as a percentage of the federal poverty level?
- Was affordable employer coverage available to you in any month?
- Did your income change during the year from what you estimated at enrollment?
## Common Errors
- Filing without Form 1095-A or skipping Form 8962 reconciliation entirely
- Underestimating income at enrollment and being surprised by full repayment in 2026
- Assuming the 2021-2025 enhanced rules still apply
- Missing that MAGI here includes tax-exempt interest and nontaxable Social Security
- Claiming the credit while married filing separately
## Prompt
- I bought health insurance through the exchange and got a 1095-A.
- I received advance premium tax credits during the year.
- My income went up and I am worried about repaying my subsidy.
