# Earned Income Credit (EIC)
## Description
Fully refundable credit for working people with low to moderate income. Because it is refundable, it can produce a refund far larger than any tax withheld. Schedule EIC appears on about 17.2 million returns (IRS SOI, TY 2022).
## How it works
Three phases: the credit PHASES IN as earned income rises, PLATEAUS at the maximum, then PHASES OUT. Both earned income AND AGI must be under the limit — the IRS applies the test to whichever is higher.
## 2026 — IRS published figures (Rev. Proc. 2025-32)
MAXIMUM CREDIT by number of qualifying children:
| Qualifying children | Maximum credit |
|---|---|
| None | $664 |
| One | $4,427 |
| Two | $7,316 |
| Three or more | $8,231 |
INVESTMENT INCOME LIMIT: $12,200
The maximum credit is the SAME for every filing status. Only the income limits differ.
The complete 2026 table of earned income and AGI limits by filing status is in Rev. Proc. 2025-32 and will appear in the Schedule EIC instructions and Publication 596. As of this writing the IRS EITC Central income-limit page still shows tax year 2024, so use Rev. Proc. 2025-32 rather than a secondary summary for a borderline case.

## How filing status changes the numbers
- The MAXIMUM CREDIT is the same for every filing status. Only the income limits move
- Married filing jointly gets a fixed amount added to the phase-out threshold under IRC Section 32(b)(2)(B). In 2025 that addition was $7,110 for the no-children category and $7,120 for each category with children. It is a FLAT ADDITION, not a doubling — a married couple does not get twice the income room that two single filers would
- Head of household and qualifying surviving spouse use the SAME limits as single
- MARRIED FILING SEPARATELY is barred, apart from a narrow exception under the American Rescue Plan Act rule for certain separated spouses living apart
## The investment income cliff
2026 limit: $12,200 (2025: $11,950). This is a CLIFF, not a phase-out. One dollar over and the ENTIRE credit is lost, regardless of how low earned income is. Investment income here includes taxable AND tax-exempt interest, dividends, net capital gains, and net rental and royalty income. A single year-end capital gain distribution from a mutual fund can wipe out the whole credit.
## The hidden marginal rate
In the phase-out range the credit falls by about 16% (one child) to 21% (two or more children) of each additional dollar earned. Combined with income tax and FICA, a worker in that range can face an effective marginal rate near 40% — higher than a high earner's. This is worth knowing before advising someone to take extra hours.
## Key eligibility rules
- You, your spouse, and any qualifying children must have valid SSNs issued by the return due date
- You must have earned income; investment income above an annual limit disqualifies you entirely
- You cannot file MARRIED FILING SEPARATELY, with a narrow exception for certain separated spouses living apart
- You must be a U.S. citizen or resident alien all year
- You cannot be a qualifying child of another person
- Without a qualifying child, age limits apply (generally at least 25 and under 65)
- You cannot claim it if you file Form 2555 (foreign earned income exclusion)
## Qualifying child tests
Relationship, age, residency (lived with you in the U.S. more than half the year), and joint return tests. A qualifying child for the EIC does not have to be your dependent in every case, and the age limit is more generous than for the Child Tax Credit — under 19, under 24 if a full-time student, or any age if permanently and totally disabled.
## Notes
- Refunds that include the EIC cannot be issued before mid-February by law, which delays some returns
- Improper EIC claims carry a penalty regime: a taxpayer who claims it recklessly or fraudulently can be BARRED from claiming it for 2 to 10 years and must then file Form 8862 to reclaim it
- Combat pay can be included or excluded from earned income at your election, whichever produces the larger credit
## Required Information
- Total earned income (Form W-2 Box 1, self-employment net earnings)
- AGI and investment income
- Each qualifying child's name, SSN, date of birth, relationship, and months lived with you
- Filing status
## Questions
- What was your earned income and AGI for the year?
- Do you have qualifying children, and do they have valid SSNs?
- Is your investment income under $12,200 for 2026 (a hard cliff)?
- Are you filing married filing separately (which usually disqualifies you)?
- Were you previously barred from claiming the EIC (Form 8862 may be required)?
## Common Errors
- Claiming a child who did not live with you more than half the year in the United States
- Two people claiming the same child without applying the tiebreaker rules
- Overlooking investment income that exceeds the limit, including a late mutual fund capital gain distribution
- Claiming it while married filing separately
- Missing Form 8862 after a prior disallowance
