# Excess Social Security Tax Withheld
## Description
If you worked for TWO OR MORE employers during the year and their combined Social Security withholding exceeded the annual maximum, the excess is refundable. It is claimed as a payment on Schedule 3 Line 11. Many people never notice it.
## Why it happens
Each employer withholds 6.2% Social Security tax on wages up to the annual wage base, and each employer tracks only ITS OWN wages. Employers cannot see what another employer paid you. Someone who earns $120,000 at each of two jobs has $240,000 of wages, but both employers withhold as if their $120,000 were the only income. The result is over-withholding that only the return can fix.
## 2026 amounts
- Social Security wage base: $184,500
- Rate: 6.2%
- MAXIMUM Social Security tax per employer: $184,500 x 6.2% = $11,439
- If your total Form W-2 Box 4 across all employers exceeds $11,439, the excess goes on Schedule 3 Line 11
## The rules that trip people up
- TWO OR MORE EMPLOYERS is required. If a SINGLE employer over-withheld, you cannot claim it on the return — you must ask that employer for a refund and a corrected Form W-2
- Each SPOUSE is computed SEPARATELY on a joint return. You cannot combine both spouses' Box 4 amounts against one limit. A couple each earning $150,000 has no excess, because neither individually exceeded the wage base
- MEDICARE tax in Form W-2 Box 6 is NOT subject to this. Medicare has no wage cap, so there is never excess Medicare withholding — and above $200,000 there is an additional 0.9% instead. See federal/taxes/additional-medicare-tax.md
- Self-employment income does not create excess here. Schedule SE already coordinates with W-2 wages. See federal/taxes/self-employment-tax.md
## Notes
- Railroad Retirement Tax Act (RRTA) tier 1 tax follows the same rule and shares the same Schedule 3 line
- This is one of the few items on a return that is pure recovered money, requiring no election and no expense — but it must be claimed. The IRS does not add it for you
## Required Information
- Form W-2 Box 3 and Box 4 from EVERY employer
- Which spouse each Form W-2 belongs to, on a joint return
## Questions
- Did you work for more than one employer during the year?
- What is the total of Form W-2 Box 4 across all your jobs?
- Did you change jobs mid-year (the most common cause)?
- On a joint return, does either spouse individually exceed the maximum?
## Common Errors
- Never checking, and leaving the money with the IRS
- Combining both spouses' amounts on a joint return
- Claiming it when a single employer over-withheld, which requires an employer refund instead
- Confusing Medicare tax in Box 6 with Social Security tax in Box 4
## Prompt
- I had two jobs this year.
- I changed jobs in the middle of the year.
- Did I overpay Social Security tax?
