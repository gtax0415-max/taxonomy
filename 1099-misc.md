# Form 1099-MISC — rents and royalties
## Description
Form 1099-MISC Box 1 reports RENTS and Box 2 reports ROYALTIES. Both normally flow through Schedule E and are generally passive, which is why they are documented together here. Box 3 (other income) is documented separately at federal/income/business/1099-misc.md.
## Rents and royalties are different income types
They are grouped here because they share the same Schedule E route and the same passive treatment, not because a royalty is a kind of rent.
- Box 1 RENTS — payments for the use of real property you own. Passive under the passive activity rules
- Box 2 ROYALTIES — payments for the use of property you own the rights to: oil, gas, and mineral interests, copyrights, patents, trademarks. Passive in the usual case, with the exception below
## The royalty exception
Royalties are NOT automatically passive. Where they go depends on whether you are in the business that produces them:
- Held as an INVESTMENT (inherited mineral rights, a copyright you do not actively exploit as a business) → Schedule E, no self-employment tax
- Earned in a TRADE OR BUSINESS you operate (a self-employed author, songwriter, or inventor still working in that field) → Schedule C, and the net profit IS subject to self-employment tax
This mirrors the same business-versus-not question that governs Box 3.
## Box-to-line mapping
- Form 1099-MISC Box 1 (rents) → Schedule E, Line 3 → Schedule 1, Line 5 → Form 1040, Line 8
- Form 1099-MISC Box 2 (royalties) → Schedule E, Line 4 → Schedule 1, Line 5 → Form 1040, Line 8 (or Schedule C if earned in your trade or business)
- Form 1099-MISC Box 3 (other income) → see federal/income/business/1099-misc.md
- Form 1099-MISC Box 4 (federal income tax withheld) → Form 1040, Line 25b. This is tax already paid, not income
- Form 1099-MISC Box 9 (crop insurance proceeds) → Schedule F, see federal/income/farming/farm-income.md
## Notes
- Beginning with tax year 2026, the issuing threshold rises from $600 to $2,000 (One Big Beautiful Bill Act; inflation-adjusted from 2027)
- All income is taxable even if no Form 1099-MISC was received; the threshold only controls whether a form is issued
- If the payment is for SERVICES you performed as a business, it belongs on Form 1099-NEC and Schedule C — see federal/income/business/1099-nec.md
- Rental losses may be limited by the passive activity rules — see federal/income/rental-royalty/passive-activity-limits.md
- Schedule E rents and royalties are reported in separate columns; do not combine them
## Required Information
- Payer name
- Payer TIN
- Form 1099-MISC Box 1 (rents)
- Form 1099-MISC Box 2 (royalties)
- Whether any royalty comes from a trade or business you actively operate
- Related expenses (depletion for mineral royalties, and rental expenses for Box 1)
## Questions
- Did you receive rent for property you own (Box 1)?
- Did you receive royalties (Box 2), and are they from an investment or from a business you actively operate?
- Do you have related expenses to deduct on Schedule E?
- Are the royalties from oil, gas, or mineral interests (depletion may apply)?
## Common Errors
- Putting rental or royalty income on Schedule C when it is passive investment income
- Missing the opposite error: reporting an active author's or inventor's royalties on Schedule E and understating self-employment tax
- Combining rents and royalties in one Schedule E column
- Forgetting depletion on oil, gas, and mineral royalties
- Looking here for Box 3 other income, which is documented under business/
## Prompt
- I received a 1099-MISC for rent paid to me.
- I got a 1099-MISC for royalties on my creative work.
- I receive mineral rights royalties.
