# Enhanced Senior Deduction (2025-2028)
## Description
Additional deduction of up to $6,000 per eligible individual age 65 or older, created by OBBBA Section 70103 for tax years 2025 through 2028. Often described as the "no tax on Social Security" provision, though it does not actually exempt Social Security benefits.
## Important: this is NOT an exemption of Social Security
The OBBBA did not exempt Social Security benefits from federal income tax. Benefits are still taxed under the normal combined-income formula (see federal/income/social-security/ssa-1099.md). This deduction simply OFFSETS income, which indirectly reduces tax for many — but not all — retirees.
## Amounts and eligibility
- $6,000 per eligible individual; $12,000 if BOTH spouses qualify on a joint return
- Must be age 65 or older by the LAST DAY of the tax year. Turning 65 on January 1 of the following year misses that year's deduction
- Requires a valid Social Security number. Filers using an ITIN are excluded
- You do NOT need to be receiving Social Security benefits to claim it
- Only one spouse needs to be 65+ — that spouse claims $6,000, subject to the joint phase-out
- MARRIED TAXPAYERS MUST FILE JOINTLY. Married filing separately filers are ineligible
- Available whether you take the standard deduction OR itemize
## This stacks on top of the existing senior amounts
Three separate benefits can apply to the same taxpayer:
1. The basic standard deduction
2. The existing ADDITIONAL standard deduction for age 65 or blindness under IRC Section 63(f) — a permanent, long-standing provision
3. This NEW $6,000 enhanced senior deduction — temporary, 2025 through 2028
They are cumulative, not alternatives.
## Phase-out
- Begins when MAGI exceeds $75,000 (single or head of household) or $150,000 (married filing jointly)
- Reduced by 6% of the excess MAGI — that is $60 for every $1,000 over the threshold
- Fully eliminated at MAGI of $175,000 (single) or $250,000 (joint)
- Example: single filer with MAGI of $130,000. Excess is $55,000; reduction is 6% x $55,000 = $3,300; deduction is $6,000 - $3,300 = $2,700
- Social Security benefits, required minimum distributions, pension income, and investment earnings all count toward MAGI, so they can push a retiree into or through the phase-out
## Planning note
Because the phase-out band is narrow and the rate is steep, a single mistimed Roth conversion or capital gain can erase a meaningful part of the deduction. Managing MAGI near the threshold matters.
## Required Information
- Date of birth for the taxpayer and spouse
- Valid Social Security number for each person claiming
- MAGI and filing status
## Questions
- Were you (or your spouse) age 65 or older by December 31 of the tax year?
- Do both spouses qualify (potential $12,000)?
- Is your MAGI above $75,000 ($150,000 joint)?
- Are you filing jointly (required if married)?
- Do you have a valid Social Security number (ITIN filers are excluded)?
## Common Errors
- Believing this exempts Social Security benefits from tax (it does not)
- Confusing it with the existing additional standard deduction for age 65 — they are separate and both can apply
- Claiming it while filing married filing separately (not allowed)
- Missing it because the taxpayer does not receive Social Security (not a requirement)
- Overlooking the phase-out and overclaiming
- Assuming it is permanent — it expires after tax year 2028
## Prompt
- I am over 65 and heard there is a new senior deduction.
- I am retired and want to know about the $6,000 deduction for seniors.
- Is Social Security tax free now?
